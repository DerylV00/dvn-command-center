01 · Core Philosophy

The Environment Should Never Be Neutral

Every zone, every transition, every moment of the day should either be building energy, directing energy, or intentionally releasing it. Nothing should just be passive. This principle, sourced directly from member feedback, governs every production decision.

"It's all energy."
Zach Kepes · Growth House Member · Arizona 2026

What This Means in Practice

Build
Building Energy
Moments designed to increase intensity, focus, connection, or excitement. The environment and programming work together to create forward momentum.
DJ first song on cocktail night arrival
Walk-and-talk between sessions
Active outdoor breaks — lawn games, movement
Arrival toast — champagne already in hand
Hot seat format — high-stakes, high-attention
Direct
Directing Energy
Channeling the existing energy of the group toward a specific experience or interaction. Spatial design, signage, and staff positioning all direct without announcing.
Three bars on opposite sides — creates movement
Photo booth operator pulling guests in actively
Pre-mapped sponsor introductions
Morning briefing that answers every question
Member profile cards — staff know who to connect
Release
Releasing Energy
Intentional decompression. Not passive — deliberately designed moments where intensity drops and connection deepens. The fire pit, the mountain, the after-hours conversation.
Upper pergola fire pit zone at cocktail night
Sedona ranch — stripped-down, no agenda
Cold plunge / sauna wellness window
Closing circle — the release after four days of work
Ranch campfire at 11pm — the real conversations
» The reason walk-and-talks worked in Arizona is that movement creates a different mental state. Ideas flow differently when you're walking. The body loosens the mind. Build movement into every day by design, not by accident.

05 · Production 01 · Sedona / Flagstaff Model

Production 01 — The Mountain Property

Still conceptual — Jesse needs to source the venue. But the requirements and the philosophy are clear enough to brief against now.

What the Mountain Venue Needs to Have

•Enough bedrooms for the full immersion group — 8+ if possible. The sleep-in format is what creates the depth. Members need to actually be at the property, not driving from hotels.
•Outdoor fire pit — non-negotiable. The fire is where the real conversations happen.
•Land for activities — either on-property or nearby. ATVs, hiking, fishing, outdoor cooking.
•A kitchen Chef Shannon can work in — or the option to cook over fire for certain meals intentionally.
•Indoor session space — a room that can hold the full group for Day 2 sessions. Doesn't need to be fancy — the mountain setting is the luxury.
•Limited cell signal — this is a feature, not a bug. People being unreachable from their business for 48 hours is part of what makes the immersion work.

Why the Ranch Overnight Is the Strongest Excursion Concept

•Status layer disappears. Nobody has a competitive advantage building a fire. Nobody's business wins matter on an ATV trail. The equality creates connection that a session room can never manufacture.
•The 11pm campfire conversation is irreplaceable. It cannot be programmed. It cannot be facilitated. It just happens when the conditions are right — and the conditions are a fire, exhaustion from a physical day, and people who've just spent 48 hours doing real work together.
•It scales differently. A day trip excursion is a nice addition. An overnight ranch experience is something people tell their friends about for years. The word-of-mouth value alone justifies the logistical complexity.
•It deepens the Day 3 inner circle concept. The 8-figure tight group doing a ranch overnight together is a completely different product than the full group doing an ATV day. Consider whether the ranch becomes the format for Day 3 specifically.
⚙ Decision needed: Does the mountain ranch replace the Scottsdale mansion for Days 1–4, or is this a separate Sedona event entirely? The split model (mountain for the work, mansion for the celebration) is the strongest vision. But it requires sourcing a venue in Sedona/Flagstaff that can hold the full group. Jesse to advise on appetite for this model.
06 · Production 02 · Cocktail Night Amplified

Production 02 — Cocktail Night Maximum Impact

Everything that can be added to this production to turn it from a good party into something members talk about for years. Layered by impact and feasibility for October 2026.

» Production 02 Cocktail Night now runs on three floors: ground level outdoor, great room interior (LED ceiling), rooftop (opens 9pm), and the speakeasy (timed reveal 8pm). The energy arc is built around two timed reveals — not just one party space.

The Arrival Moment — Non-Negotiable

•Champagne in every hand before Jesse speaks. Not offered. Already there. The moment members arrive from the mountain, something is waiting for them. The transition from raw to celebration should be instantaneous and physical.
•DJ already at arrival energy level. The first song they hear walking in sets the entire tone. The DJ needs a specific brief on what this moment should feel like — not background music, not peak energy — the exact vibe of arrival after four days in the mountains.
•Pool lighting running. The blue/purple glow should be the first visual. This property was designed to look extraordinary at night — let it do that job.

Entertainment Layers — October 2026

•DJ — primary energy driver all night. Quality over cost. This hire matters more than almost anything else on the production budget.
•Lawn game tournament — cornhole bracket, giant Jenga. Post the bracket publicly. Floor staff runs it. This group is competitive and will engage hard. Creates natural grouping between people who don't know each other.
•Fire pit zone — upper pergola, already exists. Add a cigar station. This becomes the best zone at the party for the people who want conversation over dancing. The contrast with the DJ zone is intentional.
•Outdoor TV — same-day highlight reel. Amys shoots during the mountain days. Deryl or Amys produces a 3–5 min highlight edit of the week and it plays on the outdoor TV during cocktail night. Members see themselves. It amplifies the emotional high of the whole event. This is the unexpected moment.
•Photo booth — Growth House branded backdrop on the moss wall. Digital delivery same night. Operator actively pulling groups in.
•Bottle service rounds — every 30–45 min, curated, through all zones. Feels like hospitality, not service.

Future Additions — April 2027 and Beyond

•Live performer — a saxophonist or acoustic performer layered over the DJ set for the first hour creates an arrival energy that is completely distinctive. One musician changes the entire feel.
•Custom cocktail menu named for the week's theme — each event has a theme, the cocktails reflect it. Members order "The Montana Build" or "The Sedona Summit." Storytelling in the bar.
•Separate venue option — Mercedes dealership, rooftop, private club. When cocktail night grows to 150–200+, the mansion stays for the immersion and cocktail night moves to a venue that can hold the scale.
•Member commitment wall — 90-day commitments printed and displayed for the group to see. Public accountability is ten times more powerful than private accountability for this group.
07 · Production 01 · Excursion Options

Production 01 — Immersion Excursion Menu

Ranked by depth of experience and bonding potential. All can work. Not all are equal. The ranch overnight at the top is not close.

Tier 1 · Transformational
Sedona Ranch Overnight
Ranch property in Sedona or Flagstaff. ATVs, fishing, hiking, building a fire, cooking food over the fire. Stay the night. No agenda after dinner. Just people around a fire.
This is where the event becomes legendary. The 11pm fire conversation cannot be manufactured. It just happens when the conditions are right — and only an overnight creates those conditions. Nobody forgets it.
Tier 2 · High Impact
Navy SEAL Training Day
Full-day physical and mental challenge run by former SEAL operators. Team-based challenges, obstacle courses, shared adversity.
Shared adversity is the fastest path to brotherhood. When you've suffered alongside someone, the relationship changes permanently. This group will tell this story forever.
Tier 2 · High Impact
Sedona Day Trip — ATV + Landscape
Day convoy to Sedona. ATV rentals through the red rock landscape. Optional hike to a viewpoint. Dinner in Sedona before return.
Sedona is one of the most visually stunning places in America. Doing it on ATVs with this group, followed by a meal with that backdrop — this photographs extraordinarily well and creates a shared visual memory.
Tier 3 · Solid Addition
Jet Ski / Lake Day
Lake Pleasant or Saguaro Lake. Jet ski rentals, boat optional, outdoor lunch on the water.
High fun, high energy, good photography. Less depth of bonding than the options above but excellent for a Day 4 excursion if the event is in the Phoenix area rather than Sedona.
Tier 3 · Solid Addition
Desert Off-Road / Dune Buggy
Guided off-road tour through the Sonoran Desert. Dune buggies or guided 4WD. Sunset timing for visual impact.
Desert landscape at sunset is dramatic and photogenic. Good for groups who want high-energy adventure without the full commitment of an overnight.
Tier 3 · Solid Addition
Hot Air Balloon at Sunrise
Sunrise balloon flight over the desert. Champagne landing. 2–3 hours total. Back for breakfast.
Visually unforgettable and completely unexpected from a Growth House event. Shared silence watching the desert from 1000 feet creates a contemplative bonding moment unlike anything else on this list.

08 · Both Productions · Group Analysis

Who This Group Actually Is

Two lenses: the member base (who they are and what they need) and the event dynamics (how they actually behaved). Both inform design decisions.

Member Base Analysis

What They've Built
Operators, Not Aspirants
This is not a group early in their journey. Josh Mettle (mortgage), James Helm (real estate development), Gideon Spencer, Devin Burr, Ramon Casaus — these are people who have built something real. They've been in rooms. The fact that they chose to be here means they're looking for something the rooms they can already access don't give them.
What They're Looking For
Depth Over Breadth. Realness Over Polish.
Zach calling out walk-and-talks specifically is a signal about the whole group. He didn't want more sessions — he wanted movement and unstructured conversation. The hot seat format landing well confirms it: they want to be seen, challenged, and held accountable by people they respect. They can get polished production anywhere. They can't get this depth.
The Stratification
Two Groups With Different Needs
The fact that Jesse created Day 3 as a separate inner circle tells you there's a stratification in the group that matters. Some members are there to grow into the room. Some are already the room. Both need to feel like the event was built for them — but the design solutions are different. Don't confuse the two.
How They Engage
They Commit to What's Pre-Primed
The portrait studio invisibility is a member behavior insight: these are high-achievers who don't wander into things — they commit to things they've been primed for. The same pattern applies to every activation. Pre-prime everything. Tell them what's happening before they arrive. The intake form, the welcome email, the 48-hour reminder — these are priming tools as much as logistics tools.

Event Dynamics Analysis

What Worked
Unstructured Moments + Physical Challenge
Energy was highest in unstructured moments and during any physical activity. Walk-and-talks were spontaneous and worked. Hot seats landed. The outdoor time after sessions was where the real connections formed. Anything that involved movement or shared physical experience outperformed passive programming.
What Didn't Work
Session Rooms That Ran Long + Wrong Cocktail Night Placement
Sessions that ran long lost the room. Passive indoor breaks didn't reset energy. Cocktail night mid-event was the biggest structural problem — it pulled people out of the mastermind headspace before the work was complete. The move to Saturday was the right call.

The Key Insight

This group bonds fastest when they're doing something together that's slightly outside their comfort zone. The status layer disappears. Nobody has a competitive advantage on an ATV trail.

09 · Top 10 Improvements

Top 10 Experience Upgrades

Specific, actionable, prioritized. Each one addresses a real gap identified from Arizona or the April 23 debrief call.

01
Add a relationship-building intake question
Current intake asks what they're working through. Add: "Is there someone in a specific industry or with a specific background you'd love to be connected with this weekend?" That answer lets Deryl and Jesse engineer 2–3 high-value introductions per person — which is what members are actually hoping for when they show up.
02
Outdoor TV same-day highlight reel
Amys shoots during mountain days. A 3–5 minute highlight edit plays on the outdoor TV at cocktail night. Members see themselves reflected back. This is the unexpected emotional peak of the entire event and it costs almost nothing beyond the edit time.
03
Give Day 3 Immersion a distinct identity
Day 3 currently feels like a smaller Day 2. It should feel like an invitation. Different room setup, different energy, possibly a different location on the property or the ranch. The inner circle should feel like they've been let into something — not just kept behind when others leave.
04
Mandate active outdoor breaks — 30 min minimum, by design
Not optional, not announced as breaks — built into the schedule as "outdoor strategy sessions." The framing matters. These are not breaks from the work; they are a different format of the work. The lawn games, the walk-and-talks, the movement — it all stays in the Growth House container.
05
Build a lightweight member wins layer between events
A private Telegram channel or community thread where the 30/60/90 day commitment wins are shared publicly with the group. This group is motivated by accountability to people they respect. Public wins between events extend the community beyond the 4-day format. Jesse notified on every win, responds personally.
06
The ranch overnight excursion as the Day 4 format
Replace the day-trip excursion model with a ranch overnight at Sedona or Flagstaff. Or make the entire immersion a mountain property event with the mansion as the closing night. The overnight is where the event becomes legendary — the campfire conversation at 11pm cannot be manufactured in a day trip.
07
Pre-map the lawn game tournament for cocktail night
Cornhole bracket posted at the entrance to the lawn zone. Floor staff runs the bracket. This group is competitive and will engage hard — it creates natural grouping between people who don't know each other, extends time outside away from the DJ zone, and gives Amys incredible candid content.
08
Cigar station at the upper pergola fire pit zone
The fire pit is already there. Adding a curated cigar selection makes the upper zone a destination, not a coincidence. The best conversations of cocktail night will happen here. It extends time in the space and creates a specific zone for a specific type of interaction — quieter, more reflective, more personal.
09
Frame the convoy from mountain to mansion intentionally
The 2-hour drive from Sedona/Flagstaff to Scottsdale should not be a logistics problem — it should be part of the program. Debrief the mountain days in the vehicles. Set the intention for the final night. Arrive as a unit. The first person who sees the mansion lit up should be able to feel the reward of it. Brief Jesse on framing this moment.
10
Excursion preference in member intake
"What type of adventure energizes you most?" in the intake form. The answer shapes which excursion Jesse selects for that event cycle. Different groups will want different things. This also tells you something important about who each member is — the person who picks "campfire and fishing" is different from the person who picks "Navy SEAL training."
